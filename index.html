Module Overview

A standards-aligned module on the
biology of cancer

Students learn how mutations in cell cycle checkpoint genes lead to uncontrolled cell division — and why that's the biological basis of cancer — through a flipped classroom sequence anchored to real St. Jude research.

NGSS Standards

HS-LS1-4 Use a model to illustrate the role of cellular division (mitosis) and differentiation in producing and maintaining complex organisms.
HS-LS3-2 Make and defend a claim based on evidence that inheritable genetic variations may result from mutations or errors in mitosis.

Learning Goals

1
Explain how cell cycle checkpoints regulate division and what happens when they fail
2
Distinguish between proto-oncogenes and tumor suppressor genes and connect mutations in each to cancer
3
Analyze how uncontrolled cell growth relates to real pediatric cancer cases at St. Jude
4
Connect cellular biology to St. Jude's targeted treatment research and genomic data

St. Jude Connection

This module lives inside the mission

Every St. Jude reference in this module is grounded in real research, real data, and real patient biology — not added for optics.

Research Anchor

TP53 Mutations

Students analyze TP53 mutations — a tumor suppressor gene studied extensively at St. Jude in pediatric solid tumors and leukemias. Not a textbook example. An actual research focus.

Real Data

St. Jude Cloud

Lesson 4 uses stjude.cloud/ — a free, publicly accessible genomic data portal from real pediatric cancer patients. Students interact with actual research infrastructure.

Patient Narrative

Meet Maya

The case study is built on the real biology of ALL — lymphoblasts, bone marrow, tumor suppressor mutations in lymphocyte development. Fictional patient, real science.

Treatment Bridge

CAR-T Immunotherapy

Lesson 4 introduces St. Jude's CAR-T cell therapy — a direct application of the cellular biology students spent three lessons building. The module earns its St. Jude connection at every step.

St. Jude research has brought the pediatric ALL survival rate from under 10% in the 1960s to over 90% today. That number is how this module closes.
Instructional Design

Every decision has a reason

Key design choices — and the research that supports them.

01

Maya is the instructional anchor — not decoration

Merrill's First Principles argue that component skills are acquired most durably when taught in the context of a real problem. Every document connects back to Maya so the summative assessment feels like transfer, not a new task.

Merrill, M.D. (2002). First principles of instruction.
02

Analogies are structural scaffolding, not style

Sweller's cognitive load theory establishes that learners lacking prior schemas must work harder to process new content. The car brake/accelerator analogy gives 9th graders a mental structure before they encounter the biology.

Sweller, J. (2019). Cognitive load theory and educational technology.
03

The teacher guide treats exit data as instructional input

Harris & Jones define formative assessment as measurement that occurs while there is still time to adjust instruction. The triage table makes that adjustment actionable — the teacher knows what to do before students walk in.

Harris, L. & Jones, M.G. Measuring student learning.
04

The simulator is application, not presentation

A video of the cell cycle is Show. The simulator is Do. Merrill is explicit: remembering information is insufficient. Students control the scenario, step through consequences, and encounter real-time outcomes.

Merrill, M.D. (2002). First principles of instruction.
05

ARCS model runs through the entire digital lesson

Attention (pediatric cancer statistic), Relevance (junior researcher framing), Confidence (immediate quiz feedback with explanations), Satisfaction (exit ticket closes the loop to next-day class).

Keller, J.M. (1987). Development and use of the ARCS model.
06

Guided notes scaffold without removing demand

Fill-in frames offload organizational cognitive work without removing conceptual demand. Students must retrieve and apply vocabulary — not copy from a completed answer. Two support levels: vocabulary scaffolds and open analysis rows.

Belland, Kim & Hannafin (2013). Scaffolds for PBL environments.
